Social Construction of Gender and Sexuality

    Gender as a Social Construct:
  • Gender is not fixed or biologically determined, but socially constructed. Masculine and feminine norms are ideologies passed down through history, shaped by culture, media, and social expectations. These norms assign power and authority, often placing masculinity above femininity and reinforcing patriarchal structures.


    Fluidity and Social Expectations:
  • Gender is fluid, and individuals "do gender" by acting according to societal expectations. Gender fluidity challenges rigid categories and embraces the inclusion of nonbinary identities. For example, gender roles are not universally the same across cultures or time periods, as they are shaped by social, political, and historical factors.


    Gender and Power:
  • Gender ranking assigns power, resources, and authority to specific genders. Masculinity is often privileged over femininity, with traditional gender binaries (man/woman) enforcing power dynamics. Genderism also reflects a societal preference for these binaries over nonbinary identities. Gender norms shape not only individual behavior but also systemic structures, contributing to the marginalization of nonbinary and transgender people.


    Social Construction of Gendered Behaviors:
  • From an early age, children learn gender labels and respond to the different expectations based on gender. Color coding (pink for girls and blue for boys) and clothing choices reinforce societal ideas about gender. These practices highlight how gender roles and behaviors are socially constructed, rather than biologically predetermined.


Intersectionality and Identity

    Intersectionality
  • This concept explores how various aspects of identity (gender, race, class, sexual orientation, etc.) intersect and create unique experiences of privilege or oppression. For example, Black lesbians face different forms of oppression than white lesbians, shaped by both their race and sexuality.


    Gender and Sexuality as Intersecting
  • The intersections of gender and sexuality are complex. For example, lesbians are often presumed to lack femininity because they are assumed to want to be like men. Transgender people may face additional challenges due to their nonconforming gender identities, and these challenges are compounded by race, class, and other factors.


    Queer Identity
  • The term "queer" serves as an umbrella term, critiquing the rigid categories of straight, gay, lesbian, and bisexual identities. It’s an intersectional concept, as it involves multiple identities and rejects societal pressures to fit into binary gender categories. Queers are often defined by their rejection of norms and the embrace of diverse sexualities and gender expressions.


    Masculinity and Femininity
  • Norms around masculinity include traits like strength, independence, control, and competence, while femininity is often associated with passivity, emotionality, and domesticity. These roles are not inherent to individuals but are shaped by culture, tradition, and social norms.


Sexuality and Relationships

    Sexual Identity and Desire
  • Sexual identity refers to a person’s sense of sexual desires and behaviors. It is influenced by societal scripts and expectations. Sexual scripts are the culturally established guidelines that dictate how individuals should feel and behave in sexual relationships.


    Sexual Identity Terms
  • Terms like "heterosexuality," "homosexuality," "pansexuality," "bisexuality," and "polysexuality" describe different sexual orientations and preferences. These categories challenge the binary gender and sexual identities, with "pansexuality" (attraction to all genders) refusing to conform to normalized categories.


    Polyamory and Nonmonogamy
  • Polyamory refers to consensually having multiple intimate relationships, while "polysexuality" refers to attraction to more than one gender. These practices reject the idea that relationships should be monogamous or confined to binary sexual categories.


    Coming Out and Identity Disclosure
  • "Coming out" is the process of openly identifying as gay, lesbian, queer, or transgender. "In the closet" refers to remaining silent about one’s sexual or gender identity. Both processes involve recognition and personal transformation, with "coming out" serving as a public affirmation of one’s identity.


Media and Cultural Norms

    The Role of Media
  • Media plays a significant role in both reflecting and reinforcing dominant gender, sexuality, and beauty norms. The "male gaze" refers to the objectification of women in media, where they are portrayed through a male perspective, often reinforcing traditional gender roles.


    Enlightened Sexism
  • This term refers to the idea that, because women have gained more rights, it’s now acceptable to present women in traditional, stereotypical roles. It’s framed as "entertainment," but it undermines real progress toward gender equality.


    Social Media and Beauty Standards
  • Social media influences how individuals perceive and perform gender and beauty norms. Social platforms often emphasize beauty standards, making them more prevalent in everyday life. This digital space serves both to reinforce societal standards and to create counter-narratives, especially in the context of activism.


    Impact of Corporate Control
  • A few large companies own the majority of television networks, controlling much of the media landscape. This consolidation of power shapes the narratives and messages people receive, often reinforcing hegemonic ideals and stereotypes about gender and sexuality.


Health and Reproductive Justice

    Reproductive justice
  • This concept links bodily autonomy and the right to make decisions about one’s body to broader social justice issues. It emphasizes the importance of equitable access to reproductive healthcare for marginalized groups, including low-income people and people of color.


    Structural Violence and Health Inequality
  • Structural violence refers to societal systems (poverty, poor housing, lack of education) that contribute to health disparities. These systems create environments that contribute to chronic stress, health problems, and poor access to healthcare.


    Assisted Reproductive Technologies (ARTs)
  • ARTs include methods like in vitro fertilization and surrogacy, where eggs are fertilized outside the body, and embryos are implanted later. These technologies raise ethical questions about the definition of life, especially in contexts of religious or moral opposition.


    Access to Healthcare
  • The Affordable Care Act (ACA) helped increase healthcare access for marginalized populations and addressed gender- and race-based discrimination in healthcare. However, healthcare disparities still exist, particularly for LGBTQ+ individuals and people of color.


Power and Systems of Oppression

    Gendered Power Structures
  • Social structures perpetuate systems of oppression through institutional and interpersonal power dynamics. Patriarchy, heteronormativity, and racism are examples of systems that maintain power and control through gendered, racialized, and sexualized practices.


    Racist Heteropatriarchy
  • This refers to the dominance of a gender binary system that prioritizes heterosexuality while simultaneously normalizing racism. These intersecting power structures enforce a particular version of gendered and sexual identity that marginalizes people who don’t fit these norms.


    Imperialism and Colonialism's Impact on Sexual Power
  • Historical systems like colonialism and imperialism shaped sexual norms by defining indigenous sexual practices as "primitive" or "uncivilized." These powers imposed European sexual norms, which continue to influence global understandings of sexuality today.


    Globalization and Cultural Norms
  • Globalization has spread Western ideals of sexuality and gender across the world, creating both new opportunities for change and reinforcing inequalities. Media, technology, and corporate power play significant roles in shaping and reinforcing these globalized norms.
