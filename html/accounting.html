Capital Structure and Financing Decisions

    1.1 Understanding Capital Structure:
  • How companies determine the balance of liabilities and equity to finance assets.
  • Role of collateral and risk in deciding between debt and equity financing.


    1.2 Debt Financing:
  • Benefits:
    • Quick access to funds, allows fast business growth.
    • Low interest rates when collateral is available.
    • Tax advantages: Interest payments are deductible.
    • Retains ownership - no equity given to the lender.
  • Costs:
    • Repayment of the borrowed principal is mandatory.
    • Rigid interest payments on a set schedule.
    • Debt covenants can restrict business flexibility.
    • Risk of losing collateral or bankruptcy.


    1.3 Equity Financing
  • Benefits:
    • No repayment of funds or mandatory interest payments.
    • No risk of bankruptcy or debt-related restrictions.
    • No collateral pledged, no debt covenants.
  • Costs:
    • Time and resources needed to raise equity capital.
    • Giving up a portion of ownership in the company.
    • Dividends paid to equity holders are not tax-deductible.


The Balance Sheet: Structure and Measurement

    2.1 Understanding the Balance Sheet Equation
  • Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Owner’s Equity.
  • Purpose: Represents the company's financial position at a specific point in time.
    • Assets reflect resources owned or controlled by the company (e.g., cash, inventory, equipment).
    • Liabilities represent obligations to external parties.
    • Owner's equity is the residual interest in the company after liabilities are subtracted from assets.


    2.2 Liabilities: Short-Term and Long-Term
  • Current Liabilities
    • Accounts Payable (AP): Amounts owed to suppliers for goods and services. No interest is charged.
    • Accrued Liabilities: Expenses incurred but not yet paid (e.g., wages, rent, utilities, taxes).
    • Short-term Debt: Loan amounts with original maturities less than a year; includes interest charges.
    • Current Portion of Long-term Debt: The part of long-term loans due within the year.
    • Deferred Revenue: Customer payments received in advance for goods/services to be delivered (e.g., subscription services, airlines).
  • Non-Current Liabilities:
    • Long-term Debt: Loans with maturities extending beyond one year.
    • Operating Lease Liabilities: Lease obligations due in future periods (details beyond course scope).


    2.3 Owner's Equity Accounts
  • Contributed Capital: Investment by Shareholders
    • Types include common stock, preferred stock, and additional paid-in capital.
    • Reflects the funds contributed directly by investors in exchange for ownership.
    • Remains on the balance sheet for the life of the company.
  • Earned Capital / Retained Earnings
    • Measured as all net income since the company began minus all dividends paid.
    • Increases with profits (net income) and decreases with dividends or net losses.
    • Represents reinvested earnings used to fund operations, pay down debt, or fuel growth.
  • Other Equity (AOCI)
    • Captures unrealized gains/losses from items not included in net income (beyond scope of this course).
    • Examples may include foreign currency translation adjustments or unrealized gains on investments.


The Income Statement: Components and Analysis

    3.1 The Income Statement Equation
  • Equation: Revenues – Expenses = Net Income
  • Objective: Shows whether a company generates revenues in excess of expenses over a specific period of time.
  • GAAP Requirement: All revenues and expenses are measured using accrual accounting.
    • Accrual Accounting: Revenues recorded when goods/services are delivered, not when cash is received.
    • Expenses recorded when costs are incurred, not when paid.


    3.2 Revenue and Cost of Goods Sold
  • Revenue
    • Represents the total amount of income earned from selling goods or providing services during the period.
    • Net Revenue: Adjusts for customer returns, allowances, or discounts.
    • Common Names: Sales, Revenues, Net Sales, Sales Revenue/Net.
  • Cost of Goods Sold
    • Reflects the direct costs of producing or purchasing the goods sold during the period.
    • Raw materials, direct labor costs, and manufacturing overhead & shipping and handling to deliver inventory to customers.
    • Service companies do not report COGS.li>
    • Common Names: COGS, Cost of Sales, Cost of Revenues, Cost of Products Sold.
  • Gross Profit
    • Equation: Revenue – COGS = Gross Profit.
    • Indicates whether revenue is sufficient to cover the direct costs of goods/services sold.


    3.3 Operating Expenses and Operating Income
  • Operating Expenses (Indirect Costs)
    • Costs not directly tied to producing specific goods or services but incurred in day-to-day operations.
    • Selling, General, and Administrative Expenses (SG&A): Salaries, wages, marketing, rent, utilities, and facilities management.
    • Depreciation and Amortization: Spreading the cost of tangible/intangible assets over their useful lives.
  • Operating Income
    • Equation: Revenue – Operating Expenses = Operating Income.
    • Reflects profitability from core business operations before considering non-operating items and taxes.


    3.4 Non-Operating Items and Net Income
  • Non-Operating Income and Expenses
    • Items unrelated to core operations, categorized as Investing or Financing activities:
    • Interest Income: Income earned from excess cash invested in stocks, bonds, or other securities.
    • Interest Expense: Cost of borrowing, calculated as principal amount × interest rate.
    • Gains or Losses: From sale of investments, assets, or other non-operating activities.
  • Pretax Income
    • Equation: Operating Income +/- Non-Operating Income/Expenses.
    • Reflects total profit before considering income taxes.
  • Income Tax Expense
    • Taxes owed on profits earned during the period.
    • Calculated using the effective tax rate: Income Taxes ÷ Pretax Income.
    • Common Names: Tax Expense, Provision for Income Taxes, Tax Provision./li>
  • Net Income
    • Equation: Revenues – Expenses – Taxes = Net Income.
    • Indicates final profitability after all costs, non-operating items, and taxes are accounted for.
    • If positive = Net Income (Profit); if negative = Net Loss.


Transaction Analysis and Adjustments

    4.1 The Accounting Equation and Dual Effects
  • Accounting Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Owner’s Equity.
  • Dual Effect: Every transaction affects at least two accounts, ensuring the balance sheet stays in balance.
  • Examples of Account Movements:
    • Asset Increases: Offset by a decrease in another asset. Offset by an increase in liabilities. Offset by an increase in equity.
    • Liability Increases: Offset by an increase in assets. Offset by a decrease in another liability. Offset by a decrease in equity.


    4.2 Transaction Analysis During the Period
  • Balance Sheet Accounts
    • Have beginning balances that “roll over” from the prior period.
    • Examples: Cash, Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Liabilities, Retained Earnings.
  • Income Statement Accounts
    • Reset to zero at the start of each period, with balances transferred to retained earnings.
    • Examples: Revenue, COGS, Operating Expenses, Net Income.
  • Transaction Steps:
    • Identify the first account affected and determine if it increased or decreased.
    • Identify the second (or third) account affected and determine if it increased or decreased.
    • Ensure the accounting equation remains in balance.


    4.3 Adjusting Transaction at the End of the Period
  • Deferred Revenue Adjustment
    • Occurs when payments are received before goods/services are provided.
    • Adjustments: Decrease Deferred Revenue (Liability). Increase Revenue (Income Statement). Effect: Net Income increases, retained earnings increase.
  • Prepaid Expense Adjustment
    • Occurs when expenses are paid in advance but benefits are consumed over time.
    • Adjustments: Decrease Prepaid Expense (Asset). Increase Expense (Income Statement). Effect: Net Income decreases, retained earnings decrease.
  • Depreciation Adjustment
    • Allocates the cost of long-term assets over their useful lives.
    • Adjustments: Increase Depreciation Expense (Income Statement). Increase Accumulated Depreciation (Contra-Asset on Balance Sheet). Effect: Net Income decreases, retained earnings decrease.


    4.4 Accrued Transactions (Adjustments Not Yet Recorded)
  • Accrued Liabilities
    • Expenses incurred but not yet paid.
    • Wages Payable: Employees work during the month, but wages are paid in the next period.
    • Interest Payable: Interest accumulates on loans due to the passage of time.
    • Taxes Payable: Profits are earned, but taxes are paid in a future period.
    • Adjustments: Increase Expense (Income Statement). Increase Liability (Balance Sheet). Effect: Net Income decreases, retained earnings decrease.
  • Accrued Revenue
    • Revenue earned but cash not yet received.
    • Adjustments: Increase Accounts Receivable (Asset). Increase Revenue (Income Statement). Effect: Net Income increases, retained earnings increase.


Financial Statement Analysis and Leverage

    5.1 Financial Statement Analysis
  • Helps assess a company's health and performance.
  • Key users: company managers, lenders, analysts, investors, and regulators.
  • Economies of Scale: Achieve better rates and schedules due to increased size and efficiency.


    5.2 Common Sizing
  • Balance Sheet Common Sizing: Express assets, liabilities, and equity as a percentage of total assets.
  • Income Statement Common Sizing: Express income statement accounts as a percentage of total sales. This highlights margins and profitability.
  • Why? Enables comparison across companies and over time.


    5.3 Leverage
  • Increasing debt raises assets to equity, enhancing return on equity (ROE).
  • Good leverage: Return on investment exceeds borrowing costs.
  • Bad leverage: Return on investment is less than borrowing costs.


    5.4 DuPont Framework
  • Return on Equity = Profit Margin x Asset Turnover x Assets to Equity
    • Return on Equity (ROE): Measures income generated per dollar of equity.
    • Equation: ROE = Net Income / Total Equity
    • Profit Margin: Measures profitability (net income per dollar of sales).
    • Equation: Porfit Margin = Net Income / Sales
    • Asset Turnover: Measures asset efficiency (sales per dollar of assets).
    • Equation: Asset Turnover = Sales / Total Assets
    • Assets to Equity (A to E): Measures financial leverage (assets per dollar of equity).
    • Equation: Assets to Equity = Total Assets / Total Equity


    5.5 Statement of Equity
  • Contributed Capital
    • Common Stock
      • Represents ownership in the company and comes with voting rights. The amount is recorded at par value, and the amount received above par value is recorded as additional paid-in capital./li>
    • Preferred Stock
      • Preferred stockholders have priority over common stockholders for dividends and during liquidation. Preferred stock may have a fixed dividend rate. (Options are Convertible and Cumulative)
    • Additional Paid in Capital
      • Represents the amount paid by shareholders above the par value of the stock during issuance. It is recorded in the equity section and reflects the excess amount over the nominal value of shares issued.
  • Earned Capital
    • Retained Earnings
      • Represents accumulated net income that has not been distributed as dividends. It is part of shareholders’ equity and reflects the company’s reinvestment in its operations.
    • Dividends
      • Dividends reduce retained earnings and are usually declared from the company’s earnings. However, they cannot exceed the retained earnings balance without the company borrowing funds or issuing new equity.
    • Declaration Date: The date the company’s board of directors declares the dividend.
    • Payment Date: The date when dividends are paid out to shareholders.
  • Returned Capital
    • Treasury Stock
      • Treasury stock refers to shares that were once issued but later repurchased by the company. These shares are not considered outstanding and are excluded from the calculation of earnings per share (EPS) and dividends.
      • Reasons for Repurchasing Stock
        • To use the shares for employee compensation plans (like stock options).
        • To increase the value of remaining outstanding shares (by reducing dilution).
        • To show confidence in the company by reducing shares in circulation.


Cash Conversion Cycle and Managing Cash Flow

    6.1 Cash Flow Statement
  • Operating Activities:
    • Cash inflows: Includes receipts from customers, dividends, and interest received.
    • Cash outflows: Includes payments to suppliers, employees, and taxes.
    • Net cash from operating activities is an important indicator of the core operational efficiency of a business.
  • Investing Activities
    • Includes purchases and sales of long-term assets such as property, plant, and equipment (PP&E), and investments.
    • Cash outflows: Purchase of long-term assets or investments.
    • Cash inflows: Sale of assets or investments.
  • Financing Activities
    • Involves inflows and outflows related to debt and equity, such as issuing or repurchasing stock, borrowing, and repaying debt.
    • Cash inflows: Borrowing funds or issuing stock.
    • Cash outflows: Repayment of debt or dividends paid.


    6.2 Cash Gap and Conversion Cycle
  • Cash Gap: The time between cash outflow to suppliers and cash inflow from customers.
  • Formula: Cash Gap = Days Inventory Held (DIH) + Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) - Days Payable Outstanding (DPO).


    6.3 Days Inventory Held
  • DIH = Days in Period / (COGS/Inventory).
  • Inventory Turnover = Cost of Goods Sold / Inventory
  • Measures how long inventory sits before being sold.


    6.3 Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)
  • DSO = Days in Period / (Sales/Acct. Receivable)
  • Accounts Receiveable Turnover = Sales / Acct. Recievable
  • Measures the efficiency in collecting accounts receivable.


    6.4 Days Payable Outstanding (DPO)
  • DPO = Days in Period / (COGS/Acct. Payable)
  • Accounts Payable Turnover = Cost of Goods Sold / Acct. Payable
  • Measures how long it takes to pay suppliers.


    6.5 Reducing Cash Gap
  • Reduce DIH: Improve inventory tracking and forecasting.
  • Reduce DSO: Offer customer discounts, hire collection specialists, or factor receivables.
  • Increase DPO: Delay payments to suppliers (with care to avoid relationship damage).


Earnings Management and Fraud Prevention

    7.1 Earnings Management Overview
  • Definition: Earnings management involves using discretion over financial reporting and transactions to achieve desired financial outcomes.
  • Impact on Financial Statements: Earnings management allows managers to shape how a company’s earnings appear, which can affect net income reported in the income statement. While it doesn't necessarily involve fraudulent activity, it may involve manipulating accounting judgments.
  • Accrual Accounting: Under accrual accounting, managers must make judgments and estimates, such as how long to depreciate assets or how much to reserve for doubtful accounts. These estimates can be adjusted to either boost or reduce reported income.


    7.2 Incentives for Earnings Management
  • Deliver Growth: Managers may engage in earnings management to show that the company is growing, even if the growth is minimal, to satisfy stakeholders and the market.
  • Meet Stock Market Expectations: If a company is close to meeting its projected earnings, managers may take steps to ensure they reach that target, avoiding a negative market reaction. This is especially true for companies with publicly traded stocks where market expectations drive stock prices.
  • Meet Compensation Bonuses: In some cases, managers’ bonuses are tied directly to achieving specific income targets. Manipulating financial results can ensure these bonuses are met.
  • Avoid Violating Debt Covenants: Debt covenants often require companies to maintain certain financial ratios (like net income or EBITDA). If a company is in danger of breaching a covenant, earnings management may be used to ensure compliance.
  • Meet Performance Milestones: Companies may be under pressure to hit performance milestones, such as sales growth targets or other financial benchmarks, which can motivate management to manage earnings to align with these goals.
  • Increase Investor Confidence: By managing earnings, companies may create the appearance of stability and success, potentially boosting investor confidence and the stock price.


    7.3 Methods of Earnings Management
  • Accounting Managmeent: Changing Assumptions - Earnings can be managed through changes in assumptions or estimates that affect the income statement and balance sheet. For example:
    • Product Returns: Estimating a higher rate of product returns will reduce current period income, while underestimating returns will inflate income.
    • Bad Debt: Adjusting the estimate for uncollectible accounts can affect earnings. By underestimating bad debt, earnings are artificially inflated.
    • Depreciation: Choosing a longer depreciation schedule for assets can spread the expense over more years, lowering annual depreciation expenses and inflating income in the short term.
    • Inventory Valuation: Changing inventory assumptions (e.g., lower inventory write-downs) can impact cost of goods sold, affecting profit margins.
  • Real Activities Management: Changing Business Practices - Unlike accounting management, real activities management involves changing actual business practices, which can affect cash flow.
    • Cutting Research and Development (R&D): Companies may reduce spending on R&D to increase reported earnings in the short term, though this can hurt long-term growth.
    • Delaying Maintenance: Companies may delay maintenance on equipment or facilities to reduce costs in the short term, potentially leading to higher long-term expenses.
    • Employee Bonuses: Postponing employee bonuses or salary increases can boost earnings temporarily. However, it can also affect employee morale and retention.
    • Accelerating Sales: Companies might accelerate sales by offering discounts or pushing customers to order early, thus recognizing revenue in the current period rather than later.
    • Delaying Expenditures: Managers might delay planned expenses, such as capital expenditures or maintenance, to push them into the next fiscal period, artificially boosting current earnings.


    7.4 The Fraud Triangle
  • Pressure:
    • Financial pressures like having to meet financial targets (e.g., earnings expectations, debt covenants, or compensation goals) are a major driver of fraudulent behavior.
    • External pressures from investors, market analysts, or other stakeholders may intensify this desire to "perform" at all costs.
    • Internal pressures can also emerge from an individual’s personal financial troubles or company-wide struggles.
  • Opportunity:
    • Tone at the Top: The ethical climate set by upper management is critical. If management condones or overlooks unethical behavior, it increases the likelihood of fraud.
    • Lack of Internal Controls: Companies with inadequate internal controls, or a failure to enforce them, provide opportunities for fraud. This could be the result of a lack of segregation of duties, weak auditing, or oversight.
    • Collusion: Employees may work together to circumvent controls and engage in fraudulent behavior, such as falsifying records. This is often a red flag when multiple individuals are involved in covering up irregularities.
  • Rationalizaiton
    • Fraudulent behavior often arises when individuals justify their actions. Employees might believe their actions are harmless or necessary to meet company goals.
    • Rationalizations can include thoughts like: "The company is doing poorly, and they won’t miss the money," or "I deserve this because I work hard and am underpaid."
    • Even small, seemingly inconsequential acts of fraud can lead to larger schemes if the rationalization process is allowed to persist.


    7.5 Preventing Fraud
  • Internal Controls: One of the most effective ways to prevent fraud is through strong internal controls.
    • Preventive Controls: These are designed to stop fraud before it happens. Examples include segregation of duties (e.g., one person handles cash, and another records it), physical controls (e.g., locking up assets), and proper authorization (e.g., requiring management approval for large purchases).
    • Detective Controls: These controls help identify fraud after it occurs. Common methods include regular reconciliations, data analytics, and internal audits.
  • Audits: Internal and external audits are crucial in identifying and addressing financial discrepancies. Companies should conduct regular audits and ensure auditors have access to relevant records and are independent from management pressure.
  • Monitoring: Companies should implement a process of continuous monitoring and review to detect irregularities in real time, such as discrepancies in financial reporting or sales data.


    7.6 Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)
  • Purpose: SOX was implemented in response to high-profile accounting scandals (e.g., Enron, Worldcom) to restore investor confidence in financial reporting.
  • Requirements:
    • Companies must establish and maintain internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR).
    • Management must assess and report on the effectiveness of these controls.
    • External auditors must attest to the effectiveness of the company’s internal controls.
  • Penalties: SOX imposes severe penalties for non-compliance, including criminal charges for executives who knowingly certify false financial statements.
  • Impact on Fraud Prevention: SOX helps to ensure transparency and accountability in financial reporting, aiming to reduce the likelihood of fraudulent activities through robust reporting and auditing requirements.


Taxation and Corporate Tax Systems

    8.1 Federal Tax Revenue
  • Individual Income Taxes: Wages, salary, investments, and non-corporate business profits.
  • Payroll Taxes: Fund Social Security, Medicare, and unemployment insurance.
  • Corporate Income Taxes: Taxes on corporate profits.
  • Excise Taxes: Taxes on specific goods like gasoline, alcohol, etc.
  • Gift and Estate Taxes: Taxes on wealth transfers.


    8.2 Tax Revenue Allocation
  • Funds key services like national defense, social security, and education.


    8.3 State and Local Taxes
  • Property Taxes: Tax on land, structures, and personal property like cars and boats.
  • Sales Taxes: Taxes on retail goods and certain services.
  • Income Taxes: Some states and localities levy income taxes in addition to federal taxes.


    8.4 Tax Calculation
  • Formula: Tax Liability = Tax Base x Tax Rate


    8.5 Federal Income Taxes for Individuals
  • Gross Income: Total income before deductions.
  • Adjusted Gross Income (AGI): Gross income minus certain deductions (e.g., retirement contributions).
  • Taxable Income: After applying deductions (standard or itemized), this is the amount taxed.
  • Tax Liability: What you owe before credits.
  • Total Tax: After applying credits and withholdings, determines taxes due or refund.


Managerial Accounting

    9.1 Overview of Managerial Accounting
  • Managerial accounting focuses on providing information for internal decision-making across all departments.
  • Unlike financial accounting, which follows standardized rules, managerial accounting is tailored to the company’s unique needs and situations.
  • It provides flexibility with frameworks that can be adapted to specific business environments.
  • The main goal is to support decision-making, planning, and control processes within the organization.


    9.2 Product vs. Period Costs
  • Product Costs: These costs are directly associated with the production of goods and services and are inventoried until the goods are sold.
    • Direct materials: Raw materials that are directly used in the production of a product.
    • Direct labor: Labor costs that can be directly attributed to the production process.
    • Manufacturing overhead: Indirect costs like utilities, rent, and factory supplies.
    • Freight in: Shipping costs related to bringing materials into the factory.
  • Period Costs: Costs that are not directly tied to the production process and are expensed in the period incurred.
    • SG&A (Selling, General & Administrative): Includes marketing, sales, and administrative expenses.
    • Freight out: Shipping costs for delivering products to customers.
    • Corporate operating expenses: General company-wide expenses not tied to production.


    9.3 Predetermined Overhead Rate
  • A rate used to allocate overhead costs based on a chosen cost driver (e.g., labor hours or machine hours).
  • The formula is: Predetermined Overhead Rate = Expected Overhead / Expected Cost Driver.
  • Example: If the expected overhead is $100,000, and the expected labor hours are 50,000, the overhead rate would be $2 per labor hour.
  • This rate helps allocate overhead costs to products or services based on their actual usage of the cost driver.


    9.4 Profit Formula
  • The basic formula to calculate profit is: Profit = Selling Price - Variable Costs - Fixed Costs.
  • Selling price is the revenue generated per unit sold.
  • Variable costs are costs that change with the production volume, such as materials and direct labor.
  • Fixed costs remain constant regardless of the number of units produced, such as rent and salaries.


Cost-Volume-Profit Analysis

    10.1 Introduction to CVP Analysis
  • CVP analysis examines how changes in cost and volume affect an organization’s profit.
  • It helps managers understand the relationships between fixed and variable costs, sales volume, and profit.
  • The key goal is to understand the breakeven point—where total revenue equals total costs—and how various factors impact profitability.


    10.2 Contribution Margin
  • Contribution Margin (CM) is the difference between sales revenue and variable costs. It represents the amount of revenue that is available to cover fixed costs.
  • Formula: CM = Revenue - Variable Costs.
  • Contribution Margin Ratio is calculated as (Revenue - Variable Costs) / Revenue and represents the percentage of revenue available to cover fixed costs.
  • A higher CM ratio indicates lower variable costs and a greater ability to cover fixed costs and generate profit.


    10.3 Breakeven Analysis
  • Breakeven point is where total revenue equals total costs (fixed + variable costs), resulting in no profit or loss.
  • Breakeven Formula: Fixed Costs / Contribution Margin per Unit.
  • Alternative Breakeven Formula: Using the contribution margin per unit, calculate how many units need to be sold to cover fixed costs.
  • Breakeven analysis helps managers determine the sales volume required to cover costs and start making a profit.


    10.4 Target Income
  • To achieve a target income, you can adjust the breakeven formula to solve for the number of units that need to be sold to reach a specific profit.
  • Formula: (Fixed Costs + Target Income) / Contribution Margin per Unit.
  • This helps businesses set realistic sales targets and pricing strategies to achieve desired profit levels.


Budgeting & Sustainability

    11.1 Sales Budget
  • The sales budget forecasts the number of units to be sold and the expected selling price, helping estimate revenue for a given period.
  • It is the foundation for all other budgets, as it impacts production, inventory, and financial planning.
  • To create an accurate sales budget, historical data, market trends, and external factors should be considered.


    11.2 Inventory Budget
  • The inventory budget ensures that a company has the right amount of inventory on hand to meet sales demand without overstocking.
  • Formula: Units to Purchase = (Units Sold + Target Ending Inventory) - Beginning Inventory.
  • The cost of inventory purchases is then calculated by multiplying the units to purchase by the cost per unit.
  • Effective inventory budgeting helps optimize stock levels, reduce storage costs, and avoid stockouts.


    11.3 SG&A Budget
  • The SG&A budget estimates the selling, general, and administrative expenses for the period.
  • It includes variable SG&A costs (like commissions) and fixed SG&A costs (like salaries, rent, and utilities).
  • Formula: Total SG&A = (Units Sold x Variable SG&A per Unit) + Fixed SG&A.
  • Proper SG&A budgeting ensures that non-production expenses are controlled and aligned with revenue projections.


    11.4 Cash Flow Budget
  • A cash flow budget tracks the inflow and outflow of cash to ensure that the company maintains liquidity.
  • Formula: Net Change in Cash = Revenue (Cash Inflows) - Inventory Cash Payments - SG&A Cash Payments.
  • It is crucial for ensuring that the company can meet its short-term financial obligations and avoid cash shortages.
  • The budget helps manage cash timing, ensuring enough cash is available for operations.


    11.5 Sustainability in Budgeting
  • Sustainability involves considering the long-term impact of business activities, such as environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors.
  • Companies should factor in the cost of externalities (e.g., environmental damage) into their financial planning and budgeting.
  • Addressing sustainability in budgeting ensures that the company operates responsibly while maintaining profitability.


Data Analytics & Performance Measurement

    12.1 Performance Measurement Systems
  • Balanced Scorecard: A framework for measuring performance from four perspectives: financial, customer, internal business processes, and learning and growth.
  • Strategic Objectives: Short statements that define the purpose of actions taken by an organization to achieve its strategy.
  • Scorecard Measures: Metrics used to track performance against strategic objectives. These may include both financial and non-financial measures.
  • Strategic Initiatives: Actionable plans that aim to improve performance in specific areas identified in the scorecard.
  • Strategy Map: A visual representation that links strategic objectives in a cause-and-effect chain, illustrating how different aspects of the strategy are interrelated.


    12.2 Data Analytics Process
  • Ask the Right Questions: Effective data analysis begins with clear, actionable, and relevant questions. This shapes the data collection process and the way results are interpreted.
  • Extract, Transform, and Load (ETL): The process of gathering data, cleaning it, and converting it into a format that can be analyzed.
    • Structured data is well-organized, typically in tables with rows and columns (e.g., sales data).
    • Unstructured data, such as social media posts or images, requires processing to be usable for analysis.
  • Apply Analytics Techniques: Use techniques like regression analysis, trend analysis, or hypothesis testing to gain insights from the data.
  • Interpret and Share Results: Present findings to stakeholders using clear visuals, such as charts and graphs, and explain how they can inform business decisions.


    12.3 Statistics and Interpretation
  • Descriptive Statistics: Measures like mean, median, standard deviation, and range are used to summarize and interpret data.
  • Outliers: Data points significantly different from others. They can skew analysis and need to be managed by truncating or winsorizing (adjusting extreme values).
  • Standard Deviation: Measures how much data varies from the mean. High standard deviation indicates higher variability and risk.


    12.4 Correlation and Regression
  • Correlation: Measures the relationship between two variables. It can be positive, negative, or neutral. A correlation of 0 means no relationship.
  • Regression Analysis: Builds a model to predict the value of a dependent variable based on one or more independent variables.
    • Slope: The slope in a regression model shows the rate of change in the dependent variable for a unit change in the independent variable.
    • Intercept: Represents the fixed cost in a cost model.


    12.5 Visualizing Data
  • Pie Charts: Used to show proportions of a whole, ideal for displaying market share or category distribution.
  • Bar Charts: Display comparisons between categories, useful for analyzing sales by region or product type.
  • Line Charts: Great for showing trends over time, such as sales growth or market share shifts.
  • Stacked Bar Charts: Used to show proportions across multiple categories, useful for comparing sales across regions and products over time.
